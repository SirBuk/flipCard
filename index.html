Class participation: General

Make your expectations clear

  • Reassure students that speaking up during class is appropriate and expected in Australia.

  • Provide clear guidelines on how to participate. Put these up on a poster or PowerPoint slide or on a handout.

  • Show them what you mean by ‘being engaged’, don’t just tell them. The Class Participation vide may help.

  • Use role models: if one student makes a good contribution, praise them and remind the class that this is what you want.

Limit the audience

  • Put student in pairs or small groups: this often makes them more comfortable about making contributions.

  • Nominate a speaker to ‘report back’ to the class, and change the speaker each time. This way they have preparation time.

  • Provide clear guidelines (e.g. structured questions) to the groups or pairs that each student must follow in turn.

Provide warning and preparation time

Spontaneous public speaking in English and be daunting!

  • Be specific about which student you want to speak, rather than asking a question and hoping for a volunteer.

  • Give the student advanced warning about what and when they will be contribution (tell them early on or ask them in a particular order, e.g. “go around the room”).

  • Allow students time to prepare before they speak in public (a few minutes’ group discussion time can serve this purpose).

  • Ask very reticent student to read something aloud, warn them, and give them a few minutes to prepare.

  • For further information see ‘Class participation: General’ in your Teachers Handbook.

Class participation: Eliciting contributions

Motiving students about group work

For closed questions (e.g. “What is the annual profit?”)

  • Go around the class and ask each student in turn for an answer, so they know when they’re up next.

  • At the start of class, allocate questions or task to specific students, so they have time to prepare (e.g. “For each test question, David’s job is to give us the units.”).

For open questions (e.g. “What do you think?”)

  • Let the students discuss the question in pairs/groups and:

  • Go from group to group asking or thoughts while the discussion is on (providing a sense of privacy), and/or

  • When you announce the discussion, tell each group to nominate a speaker to report back (allow preparation).

Getting students to ask question in class

  • Use role models: if a student does ask a question, praise them and encourage other students to follow their example.

  • Allow time at the end of class for students to ask questions one- on-one which is less intimidating.

  • When students ask questions after class, praise them for asking and encourage them to ask questions in class as well.

Facilitating class

Ask students to record:

  • Open-ended discussion prompts may be challenging at first. Get them started with structured questions or guidelines in pairs or small groups before they speak to the class.

  • Good topics to inspire discussion: food, sport, gifts, movies, technology, shopping, controversial events involving people in or from their country, generational differences.

Facilitating group work

Motiving students about group work

  • Tie group work to their careers: in the global workplace they will need to work in multicultural teams!

  • Provide explicit, step-by-step instructions on what they must do (e.g., hold meeting, delegate, take minutes)

  • Get student to hold their first group meeting in class time, so you can supervise and assist.

Putting student into group

  • Most student gravitate towards peers from their own culture if given the choice.

  • Encourage cross-cultural communications by randomly assigning students to pairs/groups for the start.

  • If you want students to choose their groups, build in incentives for cross-cultural communication.

  • Provide specific guidelines for discussion not open-ended prompts.

Monitoring individual contributions

Ask students to record:

  • When they hold group meeting and who attended them.

  • What each person contributed during each meeting.

  • How tasks are delegated to group members.

  • What each person has done between meetings.

Writing good PowerPoint slides

Layout and formatting

  • Font size for main text no smaller than 28 points.

  • No more the two lines per bullet point.

  • Line should be spaced at a minimum of 24 points.

  • Aim for no more than 10 bullet points per slide.

  • Set your bullet points to come up one by one on a mouse click.

Content

  • Think of bullet points as headlines for you explanation and examples, not a speech cut into pieces!

  • No sentences or phrases more than two clauses long.

  • Show as well as tell. Use visuals (pictures, graphs maps diagrams) with or instead of text wherever possible.

Speaking accessible English

General tips

  • Enunciate clearly and don’t talk to fast.

  • Pause a little after each clause, stress key words to allow students time to process, watch students carefully to make sure they are following you.

  • Don’t just tell students show them: use visuals, put important information in writing or on a slide/handout.

  • Show enthusiasm for you subject.

  • Set your bullet points to come up one by one on a mouse click.

Vocabulary

  • Use straight forward, everyday English words.

  • Use simple grammar: avoid subordinate clauses, long convoluted sentences passive voice.

  • Be careful with jargon and technical word. Provide a printed glossary, or give students relevant terms in advance so they can look them up.

  • Be careful with slang and idiom. Keep them to a minimum, and explain them if you use them.

Making teaching accessible

  • For lectures give students a list of the main points to be covered, and refer to it as you speak.

  • Ask simple open questions to check understanding (doing this one-on-one or during a group activity Is less intimidating for students).

  • Directing questions at the class hoping someone will volunteer and answer (throwing to the floor”) often doesn’t’ work. See ‘Class Participation’.

  • For further information see ‘Speaking accessible English’ in your Teachers Handbook.

Writing accessible English: Assessment tasks

Structured tasks

In structured tasks the teacher provides a framework which makes it very clear what students are meant to do. Here are some examples of structured tasks:

  • Link the term with its definition.

  • True/false.

  • Multiple choice.

  • Reproduce a passage from memory.

  • Cloze tests (fill in the gap).

  • Complete the sentence exercises.

  • Complete the graph or diagram exercise.

  • Summarise a key passage in half the words.

Unstructured tasks

In unstructured tasks (essays, reports, short answer questions, oral presentations etc.) the teacher provides minimal guidelines and students create their own answers. Some students may never has done unstructured tasks before. It helps to:

  • Provide explicit, step-by-step instructions on (a)what they must do, (b)how to do it, (c)over what time frame and (d) what help and support they can access.

  • Put key words which tell students what is expected in bold and make sure students understand what they mean.

  • Don’t just explain the task orally, provide something in writing and take students through it in class.

  • Provide an annotated example of the type of assessment task you’re setting (for oral presentations, use video footage).

  • Describe what sort of answer you expect (e.g. “Write a short paragraph about…”).

Writing accessible English: General

Vocabulary

  • Write straightforward, everyday English.

  • Use simple, everyday word and phrases.

  • Use common words (e.g. “cause”) rather than obscure or formal words (e.g. “precipitate”, “instigate”, “trigger”).

  • Include only essential jargon, technical terms and acronyms. Put these in bold when they first appear, include a definition in the text or refer students to a glossary or endnote/footnote.

Layout and formatting

  • Keep your sentences short: no more than three clauses long.

  • Paragraphs should be no more than three cluses long.

  • Break long paragraphs into shorts paragraphs and bulleted lists (where applicable).

  • Put the key word or phrase which tells the student what to do in bold.


Grammar

  • Avoid subordinate clauses (“Their costs, which were vast, are…”).

  • Use the active voice (“Students misread the text”) not the passive voice (“The test was misread”).

  • Phrase thins literally (e.g. “Woolco were surprised by their 2003 profits”). Don’t use idiomatic phrases (e.g. Woolco were blown away by their 2003 profits.”).

Teaching international students: Common challenges

Class participation

  • No response to questions asked of the whole class.

  • Difficulty in getting students to speak up (ask/answer questions, make comments, etc.) during class.

  • Asking teachers questions only after class.

  • Seeking help from peers instead of the teacher.

Difficulty with unstructured assessment tasks

  • Reproducing memorised passages for textbooks, lectures in assessments tasks.

  • Inexperience with typical Australian academic requirements such as critical thinking, analysis, group work, classroom discussion, writing essays, etc.

Difficulty with group work

  • Formatting groups only with same-culture peers.

  • Difficulty collaborating on tasks.

Why these challenges are common

Most challenges stem from international students not being used to the Australian education style. Many students will:

  • Be used to one-way class, where the teacher speaks, students listen and interruption is rude.

  • Only have been asses through structured tasks.

  • Be used to education where memorising and reproducing information is central to achievement.

  • Have limited or no experience with apply theories, critical analysis, group work and unstructured tasks.

Giving feedback

General

  • Feedback should provide input which student can apply in their next piece of work

  • Show as well as tell students what makes good work. E.g. take students through an example of a good short answer in class, explaining what makes it good.

  • Encourage ore-submission, where students give you a preliminary draft for guidance before they submit.

  • Don’t just provide global feedback (general comments about the assignment); be specific in your praise and criticism.

  • Be very clear about what you expectations were in the task and where these were met and not met.

Marking

  • Explain to students how their work will be assessed. If you have a marking scheme, provide it to student (provided the language is clear and straightforward).

  • If appropriate show students an example of similar task and take them through how you would mark it.

  • Break the mark into component for English expression, understanding information covered, etc.

  • For essays, be conscious of how the students’ first language shapes how they write.d, etc.

  • For further information see ‘Giving feedback’ in your Teachers Handbook.

Setting reading

General tips

  • Remember students may take ten time as long as a native speaker to read what you set!

  • Where possible, set reading which is accessible and clear.

  • If you can, simplify difficult texts, or explain difficult terms and phrases in annotation, footnotes or a glossary.

Textbooks:

Help students identify the main themes before they read.

  • Direct students to the chapter summary, if there is one.

  • Point out sub-heading which indicate main themes.

  • If the textbook doesn’t have a summary or sub-heading, write your own for you students.

Articles

Help students identify key themes and sections. Try:

  • Taking them through one article in class, explain the sections and themes.

  • Check for difficult words phrases, and idioms and provide footnotes or a glossary if necessary.

  • Pointing them at the abstract, if it’s straightforward and clear. If it isn’t write a better one or a bulleted outline yourself and give it to students to guide their reading.

Fiction

  • Give students an overview of what they story is about.

  • Provide a written overview of plot and characters.

  • Point students at an online overview, if a good one exists..

  • Encourage them to watch the film, if a good one exists.
